## B2 · Visual Profile

| Attribute | Description |
|---|---|
| **Base Tone** | Medium-cool grey with slight warm undertone — approximately Pantone Cool Grey 7 with a faint taupe cast; anchored firmly in the grey register without reading as brown |
| **Tonal Variation** | Moderate — slightly darker grey zones and lighter grey cloud areas create organic field variation; the aggregate inclusions create micro-scale tonal punctuation throughout |
| **Primary Surface Character** | Dense fossil/shell aggregate inclusions — small circular, oval, and irregular shapes, approximately 2–8mm across; uniformly distributed across the entire panel field; this is the defining design feature |
| **Secondary Vein System** | Fine white hairline veins — 0.5–1.5mm, traversing the panel diagonally; scattered, non-dominant; add geological depth without competing with the aggregate texture |
| **Figure / Character** | Extremely high geological character — the fossil aggregate distribution creates a surface that reads as a cross-section through geological time; no two areas of the panel are identical |
| **Surface Texture (visual)** | Semi-matte / satin — the aggregate inclusions appear slightly recessed or flush with the surface; consistent finish across field |
| **Directional Flow** | Non-directional — the random aggregate distribution means no orientation dependency; easiest panel in the collection to install without joins reading |

### Visual Distinctiveness
This is the most geologically authentic panel in the collection — a surface that references Ceppo di Gré, one of Italy's most prestigious sedimentary stones, characterised by its dense fossil shell aggregate bound in a grey limestone matrix. Ceppo di Gré has been used in Milan's luxury retail and hospitality design for decades, and is currently entering the global design conversation as part of the broader movement from polished marble to raw natural stone surfaces. In the Indian context, this panel represents a category that does not yet exist in the mass market — making it both an opportunity (first-mover advantage) and a risk (requires design education).

The fossil aggregate creates a visual language that is more geological science than interior decoration — it appeals to buyers who understand stone at a material level rather than purely aesthetic level. This is the specification architect's panel, not the showroom floor bestseller.

## B4 · Scoring — 7 Dimensions

### D1 · Design Fidelity (Weight: 25%) — Score: 4 / 5
Exceptional execution of Ceppo di Gré / fossil limestone character. The aggregate distribution, size range, and colour are well-calibrated to the natural stone reference. The cool grey matrix with warm undertone accurately captures the Bergamo Alps stone DNA. The fine hairline veins are the correct secondary element — present but not dominant. Loses one point because the aggregate inclusions appear very uniformly distributed — natural Ceppo has more clustering, with some zones denser than others; the uniformity is the tell of a printed/manufactured surface under close inspection.

### D2 · Colour-Palette Fit (Weight: 20%) — Score: 3 / 5
Medium-cool grey occupies a specific but not mass-market position in India. Strong for the contemporary urban premium segment (Mumbai, Bengaluru, Pune) and the specification architecture market; less relevant for North India traditional, Tier-2 aspirational, and heritage markets. The warm undertone helps — this is not a cold pure grey. Scores 3/5 because the grey palette, however well-executed, is third preference behind white and cream in India's residential volume market.

### D3 · Veining Drama & Naturalism (Weight: 18%) — Score: 2 / 5
The design story of this panel is aggregate texture, not veining. The hairline veins are present and naturalistic but they are background elements — the fossil inclusions are the foreground. The 7-dimension framework weights D3 (veining) at 18%, which structurally disadvantages texture-first panels. Score reflects the genuine absence of dramatic veining as a design element — not a flaw in the panel but a categorical mismatch with the scoring framework's marble-centric weighting.

### D4 · Surface Finish Quality (Weight: 12%) — Score: 4 / 5
Semi-matte/satin finish is exactly correct for fossil limestone — it allows the aggregate inclusions to read clearly without the distorting reflection of gloss, and it creates the refined natural-stone surface quality that specification architects require. On-trend. Consistent across the full panel.

### D5 · Application Versatility (Weight: 10%) — Score: 3 / 5
Grey fossil limestone is specific in its application language — it works strongly in contemporary, architectural, biophilic, and industrial-luxe briefs; it is inappropriate for traditional Indian homes (pooja rooms, heritage-style residences), most Tier-2 contexts, and anywhere the brief requires warmth. However, within its application register, it is highly versatile — TV walls, foyer feature walls, commercial lobbies, luxury bathrooms, home offices, and restaurant/hospitality spaces.

### D6 · Tactile Texture (Weight: 8%) — Score: 5 / 5
The highest tactile score in the stone collection. The fossil aggregate creates a surface with genuine physical variation — the aggregate inclusions are either slightly raised or flush with slight shadow, creating micro-topography that fingers register. Under raking light (early morning, evening, directional spotlights), the aggregate casts individual shadows creating a completely different surface character than under diffuse daylight. This is the collection's most lighting-responsive surface. The haptic experience is unique — no other panel in the catalog delivers this geological tactility.

### D7 · Trend Alignment (Weight: 7%) — Score: 4 / 5
Fossil stone and Ceppo di Gré specifically are trending strongly in global specification design — Milan design week (Salone del Mobile) has featured Ceppo prominently in 2023-2025 collections. In India, the trend is 2–3 years behind European adoption, meaning it is currently in the specification architect / early adopter phase in Mumbai and Bengaluru, with mainstream premium adoption expected 2026-2028. The terrazzo-adjacent aggregate aesthetic is the broader cultural trend vector — and this panel is the premium, refined version of that direction.
